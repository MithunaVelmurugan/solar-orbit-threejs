Introduction

A galaxy is a group of stars, clouds of gas, and dust particles that move together through the universe. There are billions of galaxies in the universe. They probably formed billions of years ago, soon after the universe began.

The Sun, Earth, and the other planets of the solar system are part of the Milky Way galaxy. Most galaxies move through the universe as part of clusters, or groups of galaxies.

Size

Even the smaller galaxies are made up of millions of stars. These galaxies may be 5,000 light-years across.

The Milky Way is roughly 20 times larger than that. It includes hundreds of billions of stars. The largest galaxies contain trillions of stars.

Shape

Galaxies are divided into three main categories based on their shapes: spiral, elliptical, and irregular.

Spiral galaxies look like pinwheels. They have a central disk and two or more curved arms winding outward.

Unusually shaped galaxies are called irregular galaxies. Some appear to be the result of collisions between several galaxies.

Our Solar System

The solar system consists of the Sun and everything that travels around it, including eight planets, dozens of moons, an asteroid belt, and countless comets.

From here we'll move outward, planet by planet, starting with the one closest to the Sun.

Planet One

Mercury

Mercury is the smallest planet and the closest to the Sun. It has almost no atmosphere to hold in heat, so temperatures swing from scorching by day to freezing by night.

A year on Mercury — one full orbit — takes only about 88 Earth days, making it the fastest-moving planet in the solar system.

Planet Two

Venus

Venus is similar in size to Earth but couldn't feel more different. A thick, toxic atmosphere traps heat so effectively that its surface is the hottest of any planet, even hotter than Mercury.

Venus also spins backward compared to most planets, and it turns so slowly that a single day there lasts longer than its year.

Planet Three

Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only astronomical object known to support life.

Earth completes one orbit around the Sun approximately every 365 days.

Earth's Satellite

The Moon

The Moon is Earth's natural satellite. It orbits Earth while Earth travels around the Sun.

Planet Four

Mars

Mars is known as the Red Planet because iron oxide, or rust, covers much of its surface. It's home to the largest volcano and one of the deepest canyons in the solar system.

A day on Mars is close to a day on Earth, but its year is almost twice as long, lasting about 687 Earth days.

Planet Five

Jupiter

Jupiter is the largest planet in the solar system, a gas giant more massive than all the other planets combined. Its Great Red Spot is a storm that has raged for centuries.

Jupiter also spins faster than any other planet — a single day there lasts under ten hours.

Planet Six

Saturn

Saturn is best known for its spectacular ring system, made up of countless pieces of ice and rock. It's the least dense planet in the solar system — light enough that it would float in water.

Saturn has dozens of moons, including Titan, which is larger than the planet Mercury.

Planet Seven

Uranus

Uranus is tipped almost completely on its side, so it essentially rolls along its orbit rather than spinning upright like the other planets.

Its pale blue-green color comes from methane in its atmosphere, which absorbs red light and reflects blue back into space.

Planet Eight

Neptune

Neptune is the farthest known planet from the Sun and the windiest, with storms that can reach supersonic speeds.

It takes about 165 Earth years for Neptune to complete a single orbit — it has only made one full trip since its discovery in 1846.
